Assignment 1: Does Marketing Spend Drive Purchase Amounts?

Author

Inayat Gill (36880817)

Introduction

Every quarter, marketing teams face the same critical question: is our spending actually working? This report looks at a personalised campaign by an online retailer, where each customer received a different level of spend through ads, emails, and coupons. The goal is to understand whether spending more on a customer leads to bigger purchases. This helps the business decide where to invest its budget next quarter, rather than treating every customer the same.

Research Questions

I will be investigating the following questions:

  1. The main question - Does higher per-customer marketing spend lead to higher purchase amounts?

  2. More specifically, I aim to examine whether there is a meaningful positive relationship between marketing_spend and purchase_amount across the 150 customers in this campaign.

  3. I will also explore whether this relationship differs across customer segments (Consumer, SMB, and Enterprise), as different segment types might respond to marketing spend very differently.

Dataset Introduction

The dataset records individual-level outcomes from a single quarter of a personalised marketing campaign. Each row represents one customer, capturing how much was spent reaching them and how much they ultimately purchased. The data was sourced from marketing campaign records and contains 150 customers with seven variables each.

Data Cleaning

Before making any analysis, the data needs to be cleaned and be in a tidy format for easy and accurate interpretations.

The following changes were made to clean the data:

  • The signup_date column contained dates in three inconsistent formats (ISO YYYY-MM-DD, written Mon DD, YYYY, and DD/MM/YYYY), which were unified into a single date format using lubridate::parse_date_time().

  • The rows where signup_date could not be parsed were dropped. The rows having missing purchase_amount values (customers who did not make any purchase during the campaign period); were also filtered, as including them would distort the relationship between spend and purchase.

  • The notes column — which contained sparse labels like “contacted” or “returned” — was not used in the analysis, as it was too incomplete to be analytically useful.

After cleaning, the dataset contained 124 complete observations ready for analysis.

Dataset Description

After cleaning, the dataset contains 124 observations and 6 variables. The variables are: a customer id, signup date, marketing spend (in dollars), number of site visits, purchase amount (in dollars), and customer segment.

Marketing spend ranged from just $1.30 to $299.61, showing that customers received very different levels of investment during the campaign. Purchase amounts followed a similar spread, from $0 to $318.92, with an average of around $135. On average, customers visited the site about 6 times, suggesting reasonable engagement across the board.

Figure 1 shows that marketing spend was distributed fairly broadly within each segment. Enterprise customers saw the widest variability, which makes sense given the fewer customers and more tailored outreach. Consumer and SMB segments received more uniform treatment. There is no obvious systematic difference in spend levels across the three groups, which is useful — it means spend variation is not simply a proxy for segment membership.

Results

Figure 2 shows that there is a positive relationship between marketing spend and purchase amount across the 124 customers in the dataset. The correlation between the two variables is r = 0.79, meaning that for every extra dollar spent on a customer, their purchase amount increased by around 0.72 (R² = 0.624). This tells us that marketing spend does have a meaningful impact on how much customers spend.

Figure 3 breaks this down by segment. The positive trend is clear for Consumer and SMB customers, who make up most of the data. For Enterprise customers the relationship looks weaker, but this is likely because there are only 13 Enterprise customers in the dataset — too few to draw strong conclusions.

Taken together, the evidence suggests the answer to the research question is yes: higher marketing spend per customer does tend to be associated with higher purchase amounts in this campaign. The relationship is not perfect — there is substantial scatter around the trend line, reflecting other factors (customer intent, browsing behaviour, prior relationship with the brand) that also drive purchasing. But the direction of the effect is consistent and practically meaningful: customers who received higher spend produced, on average, larger purchases.
